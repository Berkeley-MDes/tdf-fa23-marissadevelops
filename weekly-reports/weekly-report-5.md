## Curriculum Analysis üìö

I haven't tinkered with the Photon2 and breadboard since the introductory workshop last week, but I did apply our previous photon2 workshop lesson to an educational framework that I'm studying. A quick overview:

The term *professional vision* was developed by Charles Goodwin in the 1990s, and it expanded on the guidance that teacher's provide students based on their own high-level insights. Professional vision is broken into 3 acts:

1. Coding: identifying and categorizing features within a given context
2. Highlighting: foregrounding a latent feature in the problem environment
3. Graphical representation: visual symbolization and elaboration representing solution to problem

Here is my application to our TDF lecture:

Where coding expects students to apply categorization to a given domain of scrutiny (in this instance, the microcontroller kit), we had to explore the various hardware assigned in the tool kit and identify the relevant components for building a basic circuit. 

![microcontroller kit](https://github.com/Berkeley-MDes/tdf-fa23-marissadevelops/assets/143042473/1abd4a7c-e458-4741-99b8-58d53ee2099e)

The teachers highlighted the desired profile, or mapping, of the circuit in a guided tutorial with the class. The actions conducted by the teachers were then mirrored by the students, contributing to our trust in their professional vision of the project. Where there may have been various details we hadn't considered, their attention to detail led to conditional relevance. For example, the pins of our microcontroller should never touch a metal laptop because it could fry the hardware, but as a novice, we may not have been aware of the hazard. Instead, we were guided and had various restrictions highlighted to us.
 

![17B97322-E141-42B4-BA76-9DF1F554561B](https://github.com/Berkeley-MDes/tdf-fa23-marissadevelops/assets/143042473/d86eeaff-7750-4ef4-b41b-56f2fd96dbeb)

Note: This is (clearly) photoshopped because I did not grab a photo during lecture (unbeknownst to me, it would end up being a perfect example of *professional vision*)

Additionally, if there was low visibility for certain angles of the physical hardware on the screen, the teacher used a digital template of the circuit board to highlight the key components instead. As you can see below, the rows/columns with voltage are bolded in green -- a structural process that isn't able to be visualized with physical hardware, and instead acts as conceptual learning assistance. This is a perfect example of graphical representation within a learning framework.

This exercise contributed to students' academic maturity by means of guided practice, not only through the professional vision of faculty, but also through the tangible exploration of a given technical project.

In connection to our upcoming project on Digital Ecosysyems, explorin the use of electronic textiles was a tangible example of what our group projects will be capable of. For example, we had to building block activity. We were told to structure the blocks based on a unique word we were assigned; my group tried to visualize *flexibility* with rigid wooden blocks. It feels like an oxymoron, but I immediately thought of applying the concept of parabolic curves
to the assignment (ie, creating a curve using straight lines). Here is our final product below:
